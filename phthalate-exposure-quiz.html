      About this tool This educational estimator is designed for pregnant women, those trying to conceive, and new parents. It draws on population-level research to illustrate what current science knows about phthalate exposure during pregnancy — not to alarm, but to inform and empower. Knowledge is the starting point for small, meaningful changes. All associations shown are from peer-reviewed studies.

      Why Pregnancy Changes the Picture

      The regulatory limits shown above were designed for adults. A developing baby faces a different set of risks — here's what the research shows.

      1 of 5
      The safe limits weren't designed for pregnancy
      Regulatory limits like the EFSA TDI and EPA reference dose were derived from adult animal studies — focused on liver changes and reproductive organ weight. They were not calculated to protect a developing fetus, whose biology and sensitivities are fundamentally different from an adult's.
      2 of 5
      Your baby borrows your thyroid hormones
      Before about week 12, the fetal thyroid isn't yet active. Your baby depends entirely on your thyroid hormones crossing the placenta to guide brain and body development. The main DEHP breakdown product in your body (MEHP) competes with those hormones for the same carrier protein in your blood. Studies have found measurably lower maternal thyroid hormone at phthalate levels common in the general population — at week 10, exactly when it matters most for brain formation.
      Qian et al. 2020; Yin et al. 2024
      3 of 5
      Timing matters as much as amount
      With chemicals that act like hormones, "more dose = more harm" doesn't always apply. What matters is whether exposure happens during the narrow window when a particular organ or signal is being programmed. A small disruption at 8 weeks — when male reproductive anatomy is forming — can matter more than a larger exposure at 30 weeks. Researchers found children's behavioral outcomes were linked to exposure at week 26 specifically, but not at earlier timepoints in the same pregnancy. Timing of exposure is just as important as the amount.
      Park et al. 2026; Whyatt et al. 2009
      4 of 5
      Exposure can reshape how genes work — long term
      Fetal exposure doesn't just cause immediate effects — it can alter which genes are switched on or off in ways that persist into childhood and adulthood. Think of it as the body's instruction manual being written during pregnancy; interference during that writing process leaves lasting marks. Researchers have found changes to placental gene regulation at first-trimester phthalate levels typical of women with completely ordinary diet and product use.
      Grindler et al. 2018; Rolfo et al. 2020
      5 of 5
      Replacement chemicals aren't automatically safer
      DEHTP is now widely used in plastics instead of DEHP after DEHP was restricted. Yet a 2026 study found DEHTP was associated with the same behavioral effects in children. Regulatory approval of a substitute means it passed adult-focused safety tests — it doesn't guarantee safety for fetal development. The pattern repeats: restrict, substitute, discover.
      Park et al. 2026

      Priority Actions During Pregnancy
        What Research Has Found in Real Pregnancies

        These findings come from studies that followed thousands of real pregnant women and their children. They show patterns across large groups — not certainties for any individual. Many women with similar exposures have healthy pregnancies.

        What do "more likely" and "higher odds" actually mean? ▾
        Researchers compare two groups: mothers with lower phthalate levels vs. mothers with higher levels. When a study says an outcome was "45% more likely," it means that in the higher-exposure group, 45% more cases occurred than in the lower-exposure group — not that 45% of those mothers experienced it. For example, if 4 in 100 low-exposure women gave birth early and 5.8 in 100 high-exposure women did, that's roughly 45% more likely — but still a small absolute number. The studies are telling us about a real pattern in the population, while individual outcomes depend on many factors.
        How You Compare: Typical Urinary Phthalate Levels in Pregnant Women (US)

        From the ECHO Program (n=5,006 pregnancies, 1998–2022) and CDC NHANES. These are urinary metabolite concentrations — a direct measure of phthalate absorbed by the body.

        What do ng/mL and nmol/L mean?
        These are units for measuring incredibly tiny amounts of a substance dissolved in body fluid (urine or blood).
        ng/mL = nanograms per milliliter. One nanogram is one billionth of a gram — about the weight of a single bacterium. A milliliter is roughly 20 drops of water.
        nmol/L = nanomoles per liter — a similar scale but counts molecules rather than mass, useful when comparing different chemicals.
        Why do such tiny amounts matter? Because your body's own hormones — estrogen, testosterone, thyroid hormone — also circulate at nanogram concentrations. Your cells are exquisitely tuned to respond to signals at this scale, which is why even trace amounts of hormone-disrupting chemicals are studied so carefully.

          Studies Behind This Tool
          Key prenatal research used: Trasande et al. 2024 (Lancet Planet Health) — ECHO Program, 5,006 pregnancies, preterm birth ORs.
          Marsee et al. 2006 (Env Health Perspectives) — reduced anogenital distance in male infants.
          Zang et al. 2023 — low birth weight in ART/natural conception cohort, China.
          Kim et al. 2021 — autistic traits (SCQ scores) in Korean children.
          Barrett 2019 — prenatal phthalate exposure and ADHD diagnosis, Norwegian MoBa cohort.
          Grindler et al. 2018 — first trimester placental methylome and transcriptome disruption (EGFR).
          Hoffman et al. 2025 — newborn metabolome perturbations (tyrosine/tryptophan → attention, arousal).
          Jacobson et al. (ECHO/JAMA Psychiatry) — prenatal phthalates and postpartum depression.
          Zong et al. 2015 — DEHP disrupts placental vasculature in mice.
          Barakat et al. 2018 — prenatal DEHP causes hippocampal neurodegeneration in adult offspring.
          Gardener et al. 2025 — DEHP detected in 25% of OTC prenatal vitamins (capsule form: 51%).
          Saraluck et al. 2024 — microplastics detected in 39% of human breast milk samples.
          Laws et al. 2023 — long-term DEHP/DiNP exposure alters estrous cyclicity and birth rates in mice.
          Yang et al. 2025 — DEHP disrupts Leydig cell testosterone synthesis via cAMP/PKA/SF-1/StAR.
